The Situation

A home sale.
A $300,000 decision.

Robert is 65, retiring this year, and ready to downsize. He sells his longtime home for $800,000, buys a smaller property for $500,000, and holds $1,000,000 in investable assets. The sale puts $300,000 in his pocket.

His advisor frames the question cleanly: What is the most efficient way to use that equity as a buffer — so the portfolio can stay invested and compound through every market cycle?

Three paths are on the table. All start from the same home sale. All face the same 23 years of actual market history — including three major crashes. The numbers tell the rest of the story.

05
Why the Strategy Works

Three structural advantages.
One coordinated plan.

📈

More in the Market

Path B starts with $167,000 more invested than Path A. That difference, compounded over 23 years at market rates, accounts for a substantial portion of the $573,355 advantage — before the LOC growth even enters the calculation.

🔒

The LOC Grows Either Way

The $167,000 HECM line of credit grows at 6.16% compounded annually — whether drawn or not. By year 23, it reached $686,200 available, at zero cost to Robert. The $300,000 cash buffer earned nearly nothing over the same period.

🛡️

No Forced Selling

Path C drew just $176,133 from the HECM across three crash years — covering distributions without touching the portfolio at market lows. This single behavioral advantage — staying fully invested through every recovery — is what separates coordinated strategy from conventional planning.
